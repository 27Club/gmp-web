The European Union's Temporary Protection Directive, which has provided millions of displaced Ukrainians with the right to live and work across EU member states since March 2022, is undergoing significant changes in both the Netherlands and Poland. The Netherlands has announced updated registration requirements for Ukrainian nationals currently benefiting from temporary protection status, including mandatory re-registration through municipal authorities by early 2025. Poland, which hosts the largest population of displaced Ukrainians in the EU, has introduced amendments to its Special Act on Assistance to Ukrainian Citizens that modify the conditions under which protection holders may access employment and social services. These changes reflect a broader shift across Europe as governments transition from emergency-phase protections toward more structured, long-term frameworks.

Employers in both countries should take immediate steps to understand how these policy updates affect their workforce. In the Netherlands, companies employing Ukrainian nationals under temporary protection must verify that their employees complete the re-registration process within the prescribed deadlines, as failure to do so could result in a lapse of work authorization. Employers are encouraged to proactively communicate these requirements to affected staff and provide administrative support where needed. In Poland, the amended legislation introduces new documentation requirements for employers hiring protection beneficiaries, including updated reporting obligations to local labor offices. Noncompliance with these requirements may expose employers to administrative penalties and could jeopardize the immigration status of the employees themselves.

Deadlines and transitional provisions vary between the two countries, and it is critical that HR and global mobility teams track these timelines carefully. The Dutch re-registration window is expected to open in late 2024 with a compliance deadline in the first quarter of 2025, while Poland's amended provisions take effect on a rolling basis, with certain employment-related changes already in force. Both countries are also aligning with the EU Council's latest extension of the Temporary Protection Directive, which currently runs through March 2025 with the possibility of further renewal. Organizations should monitor official government communications and consult with immigration counsel to ensure that internal processes are updated accordingly.
