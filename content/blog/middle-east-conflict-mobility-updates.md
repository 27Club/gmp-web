The ongoing conflicts across the Middle East continue to have far-reaching consequences for global mobility programs and international business travel. Several governments have issued elevated travel advisories for the region, and airlines have suspended or rerouted flights affecting key business hubs. Companies with operations in Israel, Lebanon, Jordan, and the broader Levant should be monitoring the situation closely and reviewing their duty-of-care obligations. Even destinations not directly involved in the conflict, such as the UAE and Saudi Arabia, are experiencing indirect disruptions as transit routes shift and security protocols intensify at regional airports.

Visa processing timelines for several Middle Eastern countries have been significantly impacted. Consulates and embassies in conflict-affected areas are operating at reduced capacity, and some have temporarily suspended routine visa services altogether. For employers sponsoring work permits or business visas in the region, delays of several weeks beyond standard processing times should be anticipated. In particular, applicants with pending cases at posts in Beirut, Tel Aviv, and Amman may need to explore alternative processing locations or request expedited handling where available. Companies should also be aware that biometric collection appointments have been disrupted at several locations, adding further complexity to an already strained process.

For organizations with employees currently stationed in or traveling through affected areas, proactive contingency planning is essential. This includes establishing clear communication channels, identifying evacuation routes and safe havens, and ensuring that all assignees have current and valid travel documentation for multiple destinations. Employers should review their global mobility policies to confirm that emergency provisions, including security briefings, travel insurance coverage for conflict zones, and flexible repatriation protocols, are clearly defined and accessible. It is also advisable to postpone non-essential business travel to high-risk areas until conditions stabilize, redirecting meetings to virtual formats or neutral locations where feasible.
